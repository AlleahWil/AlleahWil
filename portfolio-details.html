Details

Voltage: This refers to the electric potential difference between two points in a circuit and is measured in volts (V). Common voltages for residential buildings are 120V and 240V.
Frequency: This refers to the number of cycles per second of the alternating current (AC) and is measured in hertz (Hz). In the United States, the standard frequency is 60 Hz.
Amperage: This refers to the amount of electric current flowing through a circuit and is measured in amperes (A). The amperage of a circuit is determined by the power demands of the devices connected to it.
Phase: This refers to the number of wires used to distribute power in a circuit. Single-phase power has two wires (hot and neutral), while three-phase power has three wires (hot, neutral, and ground).
Circuit breaker rating: This refers to the maximum current that a circuit breaker can handle before it trips and shuts off power to the circuit. The rating is measured in amperes.
Power factor: This refers to the ratio of real power (used to do work) to apparent power (total power delivered to the circuit). A power factor of 1.0 indicates that all the power delivered is being used for work, while a lower power factor indicates that some power is being wasted.
Electrical service size: This refers to the amount of power that a building is capable of receiving from the utility company. It is measured in kilovolt-amperes (kVA) or kilowatts (kW) and is determined by the size and type of electrical service equipment installed.
